Scope note

Why this looks lower than you'd expect

Two things worth knowing about these numbers. First: this counts every call-out that's an oxygen/gas-flush reading or explicitly names the MAP machine, wherever that showed up in the text — not just rows already tagged "Oxygen/MAP." Second, and more important: this is an anomaly log, not a monitoring feed. Oxygen gets checked on every line far more than 231 times across two months — only the out-of-spec readings get called out on Slack. A quiet week means fewer failures, not fewer checks.

Beyond oxygen — what else the MAP machine calls about

231 call-outs by category
⚑ Not just oxygen

46 of 231 (20%) aren't oxygen readings at all — 12 are conveyor jams feeding the machine, 10 are sealing/tooling faults on the unit itself, 7 are sensor/tray-length errors at the infeed, and the rest are pneumatic-supply and metal-detector combo events.

⚑ Rarer, but slower to fix

Non-oxygen mechanical issues average 50 min to clear versus 34 min for a routine oxygen/gas-flush adjustment — about 46% longer, because these need an actual repair, not a flush-time tweak.

⚑ One failure, five lines down

On Jun 18, a single air-supply failure knocked out all 5 active MAP machines in Production Room 1 at once — the only event in this set where one root cause took down multiple lines simultaneously.

⚑ It's shift coverage, not skill

The top 3 callers split cleanly by shift — Jared Cooper and Andres Valverde are Days (80%, 57%), Elizabeth Cericola is Nights (82%). Between the three of them, someone's on the floor logging O2 reads around the clock — that alone explains most of the volume gap over everyone else.

⚑ Rank is partly one bad day

22% of Jared Cooper's total (9 of 40) landed on a single day, Jul 4. 29% of Andres Valverde's (6 of 21) landed on May 12. Elizabeth Cericola's busiest day is only 14% of hers — her ranking is the more structural one; theirs is boosted by one rough shift each.

⚑ Volume and severity run opposite ways

The 3 highest-volume callers escalated 0%, 9%, 0% of their calls. Sheanna Donnelli — the lowest-volume caller in the top 8, at 9 calls — escalated 78% of hers. The busiest reporters are doing routine oxygen checks; the quiet ones are the ones flagging real breakdowns.
